 As you've learned in prior discussions about the PDLC, it will be grouped across four phases
 - Discovery, Define, Plan, and Build and Validate.
 Here is a high-level breakdown of the purpose and outcomes from each phase, keeping in mind
 that the detailed activities will be covered in upcoming training videos.
 For Discovery, the purpose is to align on what problem we're solving and start to shape
 the potential solution.
 As an outcome, we want a validated problem definition and a foundation of analysis that
 enables confident solution definition in Phase 2.
 What this means is that we have a clearly defined and shared understanding of the customer
 problem supported by clinical user and technical insights with early alignment on feasibility,
 goals, and the foundational workflows.
 For the second phase, Define, the purpose is that we are able to enter deeper work into
 rough wireframes requirements and designs.
 As an outcome, we now have clear actionable requirements and early designs that translate
 Discovery insights into a firm solution concept.
 What this means is that we have a well-defined solution direction with refined requirements,
 validated workflows, and draft designs that align cross-functional teams on what will
 be built as well as how it will meet user and clinical needs.
 For the next phase, Plan, the purpose is to confirm readiness to start building with most
 unknowns resolved.
 As an outcome, we now have a build-ready plan with clear scope, completed artifacts, and
 delivery alignment across teams.
 What this means is that we have a complete and aligned feature definition with finalized
 requirements, designs, and a release plan that resolves remaining unknowns and enables
 engineering to begin development with confidence.
 And finally, with Build and Validate, the purpose is to execute efficiently on the build,
 run continuously, and adjust when needed.
 And then as an outcome, we have a successful feature launch validated by users, supported
 by stable performance, and rapid post-launch issue resolution.
 We also want to take this time to share a few important call-outs about the PDLC.
 As you can see, the PDLC isn't introducing a bunch of brand new work.
 In reality, it formalizes many of the activities we already do across projects at Chorus, just
 not consistently or in a clear sequence.
 The biggest changes compared to how we operate today really show up in phases one and two
 of the PDLC, and we've listed three call-outs beneath that section.
 Next we'll be bringing engineering into this process much earlier as a close-thought partner
 to the feature teams.
 This gives engineering better context on the problem we're trying to solve, and allows
 them to meaningfully shape the solution, not just execute on it.
 Second, we'll put more emphasis on creating process maps early on.
 These help us clearly understand workflows and expected system behaviors before we move
 into design.
 And finally, we'll use wireframes to align early on how screens should be structured
 before jumping straight into detailed Figma files, or Figma designs.
 This helps teams focus on the right questions, like how the system should behave when actions
 are taken rather than getting pulled too early into UI details like button placement or text.
 Finally, we want to share some key takeaways that you can carry forward as you review the
 remaining of this training.
 First, keep in mind that the process is iterative, which means that as you go through the different
 phases, there will be times when you need to revisit artifacts from a previous phase.
 As an example, you may be in phase two and then realize that you need to adjust a process
 or flow that you completed earlier in phase one.
 You can go back and make changes and then update requirements and designs accordingly,
 which are phase two activities.
 Next, there will be both activities as well as checklist items that will need to be completed
 within each phase.
 Think of activities as having an outcome at the end, such as requirements document.
 Checklist items are more akin to actions to take, such as assigning an engineering team
 tech lead in phase one.
 Next, we want to emphasize that the PDLC will always need to be completed end to end for
 a net new feature build, which means that the feature doesn't currently exist in any
 form in our core as products.
 And lastly, we want to acknowledge that this is a big and needed change in our company.
 We are aligning to a new model for how we collaborate and build products together.
 Given this change, we will learn a lot over the next few months and this PDLC process will
 continue to evolve and change.
 So think of this as the version one release and we will collect feedback from teams and
 continue to build on this process together.
